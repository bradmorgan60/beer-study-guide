Hops: Aroma and Bitterness

Hops are strobili that consitiutute the flower of the hop plant (Humulus Lupulus). Specifically, they are green, cone-shaped flowers that contain the essential oils and resin that are used by brewers. Additionally, hops inhibit bacterial growth. This natural preservative property is a key reason why brewers began using hops in the first place.

Resin contains the alpha and beta acids that create the bitterness.
The alpha acids (humulones) go through a process called isomerization, where the configuration of molecules changes, but not the chemical formula. Let's break down this process:

  1. Hops are boiled in the wort
  2. Iso-alpha acids (iso-humulones) are created during the isomerization process, which develops bitterness and solubility in the alpha acids.
  3. The longer the boil, the more alpha acids are isolmerized -> the more bitter the beer will be. ~1 hour = 25-30% of hops are isomerized
The beta acids (lupulones) are not isomerized, but rather oxidized by storing them for longer periods of time. This is where the term 'aged hops' comes from.
The essential oils create the aroma.

Hop Varieties

  • European
    • The original brewing hops
    • Referred to as "landrace" or "noble" hops
    • Floral, spicy, resinous aromas
  • English
    • Herbal, earthy, fruity aromas
  • American
    • Citrus, with undertones of herbal, resinous, and spicy aromas
  • Pacific
    • New Zealand and Australia
    • Strong tropical fruit character with citrus and floral notes
